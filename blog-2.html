The Amygdala

In this article I will be discussing what the amygdala is, how memories and stimulators, for example smells, affect the amygdala and cause autonomic responses such as fight or flight responses.

The amygdala looks like an almond-shape set of neurons located deep in the brain’s medial temporal lobe. It is known to be involved in fear responses and memory. In addition, the amygdala influences emotional processing.

The controlling glands of the Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) is mainly in the brain, the amygdala, the hypothalamus, and the pituitary in addition the adrenal glands are all connected. A primary trigger for the ANS is the amygdala, an understanding of their structure and function is lifechanging to Autonomic Nervous System Reprogramming (ANSR). For the purpose of this article we will be focussing on the extraordinary amygdala.

The earliest researcher into the amygdala is TD Lingo. Lingo’s Story is about the fascinating man who had an incredible journey in his career making some phenomenal findings in his research.

He states in his story that “for the next 30 years I dedicated myself totally to exploring behaviour from the perspective of the human brain. My staff and I looked at every available bit of scientific research and philosophic/religious literature on the subject. We ran our own short- and long-term experiments with 309 test subjects. In the end, we discovered the mechanisms to release startling new intelligence, creativity, and pleasure, inside each and every human brain. And all our findings are supported and corroborated with foundation findings by scientists elsewhere”.

What Lingo and his associates discovered was that “The amygdala is all about conditioned responses - how we respond to THREATS, and NEW UNFAMILIAR OBJECTS and DATA, as well as the known and familiar. This extends far beyond ‘see a hot flame or a snake in our path’. The amygdala also processes HUMANS and their perceived intrinsic threats”.

Lingo provides the following example: Your amygdala clicks backward when you see a human lunging at you with a club. It ‘clicks’ backward, and your primal reptile brain survival mechanisms activate (fight or flight).

Lingo elaborates further by explaining that the amygdala is not just an ON or OFF switch. There are degrees like the accelerator pedal on your car. Sometimes the amygdala switch clicks backward and your heart rate skyrockets. But sometimes your amygdala just clicks a little, like when you tell a lie to protect yourself, and your heart rate just jumps a few beats a minute.

Lingo informs that in the same way, you react - and this includes unconscious or subconscious reaction to cues - to all kinds of stimuli in your environment, including persons, places, and things. If you perceive a THREAT- your amygdala clicks backward into defensive behaviours - and this affects your cortical thinking, more often than not, in limiting rational evaluation.”

The amygdala is responsible for the storage of emotions and memories, this includes the sights, smells and the sounds of significate events as well as feelings and emotions experienced at the time. For example, is the event a threat? The amygdala signals the sympathetic nervous system to trigger the fight or flight response, click back. Or is the event pleasure? The amygdala recalls the previous experience and triggers a parasympathetic response, click forward.

Have you ever had an emotional response or recall a memory that was triggered by a smell?

For example, when I smell Lavender I’m reminded of when I gave birth to my sons. I used Lavender to help me relax and stay calm. When the scent of Lavender is around, I’m transported back to an extremely memorable and emotional time in my life. Therefore, we could say that the smell of lavender triggers my parasympathetic nervous system, click forward.

On the flip side of this are the negative effects and emotions of smell, I personally have a physical reaction to the smell (and taste) of Tequila. The smell triggers a memory and emotional response of being extremely sick from too many drinks and to this day if I were to have a sip, even with a mixer, I would feel nauseous. Therefore, we could say the smell of tequila triggers my sympathetic nervous system, click back.

As a basic survival need, it’s little wonder that the olfactory nerves connect directly to the amygdala! This is evidence of the role of the amygdala as a sensory interface, between the sense of smell and the brain library of memories associated with that smell. How amazing is that?

Both these examples are imprinted in the subconscious mind in addition to whatever was happening as the moment is recalled, this explains why a long-forgotten aroma will unexpectedly recall powerful emotions.

The amygdala can be consciously triggered, either backward or forward, by the use of smell. The smell of rotting fish will trigger the sympathetic nervous system, click backwards, fight and flight response, and force us to take action to remove ourselves from this perceived danger.

However, if we were to smell flowers it triggers the amygdala to switch energetic flow to the parasympathetic nervous system, click forward, relaxation response.

Neil Slade explains this as “Conscious control of various ‘neurobiological’ functions has been going on forever, since humans began telling jokes and engaged in singing. Every time you do something that you know will cause a positive emotional reaction in your brain (or for that matter a negative emotion), you are exercising brain self-control, and consciously altering your neuro-function.”

The subconscious/unconscious mind has learned so much about our senses since we were born. The attitudes, beliefs, education, etc that were introduced at a very early age, maybe long forgotten by the conscious mind but are still stored in the subconscious. A positive outcome of learning to ‘click’ the amygdala forward is positive emotions such as joy, creativity and problem solving. The activation of ‘feel good’ neurochemicals and the firing of the billions of neurons in the frontal lobes is described as supercharging the mind.

There you have it! The phenomenal Amygdala!
